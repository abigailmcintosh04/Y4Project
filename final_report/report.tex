\section{Introduction}
This research project aims to develop a machine learning model to investigate the fragmentation fraction of charmed hadrons, specifically the ratio of $\Lambda_c^+$ to other charmed hadrons, detected in simulated $pp$ collisions at the ATLAS Detector at the LHC.


Showing that the model can distinguish these events, as well as background events, from each other and gives a good approximation


% ---------- BACKGROUND ---------- %
\section{Background}

\subsection{Fragmentation}
Fragmentation is the process in which coloured partons produced in high-energy collisions are confined into colour-neutral hadrons. Specifically for this project, the focus will be on the fragmentation of charm quarks into their corresponding hadrons. The fragmentation fraction for a certain hadron is defined as the ratio of the number of times that hadron is produced to the total number of times that the quark is produced. It has been historically assumed that fragmentation fractions for heavy quark fragmentation are independent of the hard scattering process, and so would be the same regardless of the production environment, e.g. $pp$, $ep$ or $e^+e^-$ colliders. However, recent data from the ALICE experiment appears to contradict this assumption, finding a ``significantly larger fraction of charm quarks hadronising to baryons [...] compared to $e^+e^-$ and $ep$ collisions'' \cite{alice2023charm}. As current reconstruction algorithms are very inefficient due to requiring detection of all decay products, training a neural network to accurately and efficiently distinguish between types of charm hadron is of great interest, as it would allow more efficient calculations of the fragmentation fraction of the $\Lambda_c^+$ baryon and therefore either verify or contradict the findings of the ALICE experiment.

\subsection{Motivations}
An accurate description of heavy quark fragmentation fractions is essential for Monte Carlo event generators to accurately simulate particle production. Accuracy of MC generators is crucial due to their widespread use within particle physics, specifically within jet tagging. Detecting $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ events has not been achieved yet due to its low cross-section (3\% at $m_H = 125 \text{ GeV}$) and the similarity of it to other processes. Developing accurate jet-tagging algorithms, such as GN2, requires training on MC simulated events, meaning any large discrepancy between MC simulations and real processes could cause poorer performance of these algorithms.

\subsection{ATLAS Detector}
The ATLAS Detector at the LHC consists of four layers: the inner detector, the electromagnetic calorimeter, the hadronic calorimeter, and the muon spectrometer. The inner detector allows for the tracking of charged particles, and consists of three sub-detectors: the Pixel Detector, the Semiconductor Tracker (SCT), and the Transition Radiation Tracker (TRT). The Pixel Detector consists of over 92 million silicon pixels, and determines the origin and momentum of charged particles. The SCT consists of silicon microstrips, and performs tracking of charged particles. The TRT consists of 300,000 drift tubes, and provides information on the type of particle detected.

The electromagnetic calorimeter measures the energy of particles that interact electromagnetically, such as electrons and photons. The hadronic calorimeter measures the energy of particles that pass through the electromagnetic calorimeter and interact via the strong interaction, such as hadrons. The muon spectrometer measures the momentum of muons. The combination of the inner detector and the electromagnetic calorimeter allows the measurement of primary and secondary vertices, momentum and energy, as well as particle identification. Therefore, this project will limit its scope to charged particles, as only these particles can have their primary and secondary vertices reconstructed. When determining resolution, the parameters will be determined from which part of the inner detector is responsible for these measurements. 


% ---------- EVENT GENERATION ---------- %
\section{Event Generation}

\subsection{Monte Carlo Simulation}
Monte Carlo events are generated using the PYTHIA 8.3 Monte Carlo event generator in Python 3.12.12, via the `pythia8mc' Python package. Two proton beams (PDG ID 2212) are collided at a centre-of-mass energy of 13 TeV. Both background and signal events are generated using this method, but with different processes enabled. The processes enabled for background and signal events are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Background: $gg \rightarrow gg$, $qg \rightarrow qg$, $qq \rightarrow qq$, $q\bar{q} \rightarrow gg$, $gg \rightarrow b\bar{b}$, and $q\bar{q} \rightarrow b\bar{b}$.
    \item Charm events: $qq \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ and $gg \rightarrow c\bar{c}$
\end{itemize}

As all charm events are generated together, they do not need to be normalised by their cross-section, as this is done automatically by PYTHIA when generating ccbar events together. A minimum transverse is set at $20\text{ GeV}$ for the charm events to ensure that the events are `jet-like' in nature. Initial state radiation, final state radiation, and hadronisation are enabled, and multiple parton interactions is disabled as this describes soft scattering events, rather than the hard scattering events of interest.

\subsection{Jet Clustering}
For the charm events, a jet clustering algorithm is used to simulate the process of clustering jets at ATLAS. The tracks are clustered using the `fastjet' Python package, with the anti-$k_T$ algorithm and a jet radius of $\Delta R \le 0.4$. The anti-$k_T$ algorithm is a widely used jet clustering algorithm, and is the standard jet clustering algorithm used at ATLAS. $\Delta R$ is defined by adding in quadrature the differences in pseudorapidity and azimuthal angle between the jet axis and the charm hadron axis, and is hence defined as $\Delta R = \sqrt{\Delta \eta^2 + \Delta \phi^2}$. The choice of $R=0.4$ is again motivated by that being the ATLAS standard. In addition, before the jet clustering, an efficiency cut of $\epsilon = 0.95$ is applied to the tracks to simulate the efficiency of the ATLAS detector. As the inner detector cannot detect neutral particles, these are removed from the tracks before jet clustering.

Once the clustered jets are created, the $p_T$ and $d_0$ smearing is applied to the tracks, as described in the detector simulation section. The $L_{xy}$ smearing is applied to the jet overall, as it is a characteristic of the jet and not of the individual tracks. Then, the summary variables are created for each jet.

\subsubsection{Mean Transverse Impact Parameter}
The transverse impact parameter, $d_0$, is defined as the distance of closest approach of the track to the secondary vertex. It is a measure of how close the track approaches to the secondary vertex, and so can be used to distinguish between prompt tracks and those from the charm hadron decay. Each $d_0$ value for a track is calculated and smeared, and then the mean of the $d_0$ values for all tracks in a jet is calculated. $d_0$ is defined as follows:

\begin{equation}
    d_0 = \frac{u_x p_y - u_y p_x}{p_T},
\end{equation}

where $(u_x, u_y)$ are the $x$ and $y$ coordinates of the primary vertex, and $(p_x, p_y)$ are the $x$ and $y$ components of the track momentum.

\subsubsection{Mean Transverse Impact Parameter Significance}
The transverse impact parameter significance, $S_{d_0}$, is defined as the ratio of a track's transverse impact parameter, $d_0$, to the uncertainty of its measurement, $\sigma_{d_0}$. It is a measure of how many standard deviations away from the primary vertex the track is, and so can be used to distinguish between prompt tracks and those originating from the charm hadron decay. The mean $S_{d_0}$ is then calculated for all tracks in a jet.

\subsubsection{Transverse Decay Length}
The transverse decay length, $L_{xy}$, is defined as the distance between the primary vertex and the secondary vertex in the transverse plane. It measures how far the charmed hadron travels in the transverse plane before decaying, and therefore can be used as a proxy for charmed hadron lifetime. As $L_{xy}$ is a property of the jet, rather than the individual tracks, it is calculated for each jet and then smeared. $L_{xy}$ is defined as follows:

\begin{equation}
    L_{xy} = \sqrt{(v_x - u_x)^2 + (v_y - u_y)^2},
\end{equation}

where $(v_x, v_y)$ are the $x$ and $y$ coordinates of the secondary vertex, and $(u_x, u_y)$ are the $x$ and $y$ coordinates of the primary vertex.

\subsubsection{Jet Transverse Momentum}
The jet transverse momentum, $p_T$, is defined as the sum of the transverse momenta of all detected tracks in the jet.

\subsubsection{Number of Tracks}
The number of tracks is simply the number of tracks in the jet. As the ATLAS inner detector cannot detect neutral particles, this can be used as a proxy for charge, as neutral charmed hadrons decay to an even number of charged particles (assuming each particle has a relative charge of $\pm 1$), and conversely charged charmed hadrons decay to an odd number of charged particles. This is helpful to distinguish between $D^0$ and $D^+$ decays, due to their similar masses and lifetimes.

\subsubsection{Jet Mass}
The jet mass is defined as the sum of the invariant masses of all tracks clustered in the jet. 

\subsubsection{Fractional Transverse Momentum}

\subsection{$d_0$ significance cuts}
$d_0$ significance is defined as the ratio of the track's $d_0$ to the uncertainty in $d_0$, $\sigma_{d_0}$. It describes how many standard deviations away from the beam axis the track is. $d_0$ significance was chosen as opposed to $d_0$ as it takes into account the detector resolution by inclusion of the error, and so will work across a wider range of $p_T$. As tracks that originated from the primary vertex will have a near-zero $d_0$, they will therefore be normally distributed around the primary vertex. However, secondary vertices will be distributed around the secondary vertex, and so many tracks will have a large $d_0$ significance, many standard deviations away from the primary vertex. Therefore, only calculating summary variables from tracks with a $d_0$ signiificance greater than a certain value ensures as many prompt tracks as possible are excluded. However, this will also exclude tracks from the secondary vertex that happened to pass close to the primary vertex, and so a balance is necessary when finding a value of $d_0$ significance cut, otherwise too many tracks from the charmed hadron decay will be excluded and measurements will be less accurate.

The machine learning model was analysed across a range of $d_0$ significance cuts, training the model on samples of 1,000,000 charm-only events for each cut, with values ranging from 1 to 5 in increments of 0.5. It was found that a cut of 3.5 provided the highest validation accuracy, and therefore was chosen as the $d_0$ significance cut for the rest of the project. All summary variables were calculated using tracks that passed this $S_{d_0}$ threshold.


\subsection{Detector Simulation}

In an effort to simulate how the ATLAS detector responds to tracks, as opposed to using truth information, two effects are simulated: smearing (of $p_T$, $L_{xy}$ and $d_0$) simulating detector resolution, and detector efficiency. 

\subsubsection{Detector Resolution}
To simulate smearing in the detector, the following equations were used:

\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{d_0} = \sqrt{a_1^2 + \left(\frac{b_1}{p_T}\right)^2} ;
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{p_T} = p_T \sqrt{\left(\frac{a_2}{p_T}\right)^2 + b_2^2} ;
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{L_{xy}} = \sqrt{a^2 + \left(\frac{b}{p_T}\right)^2}.
\end{equation}

In all three equations, $a_i$ are determined by the intrinsic resolution of the detector, and $b_i$ are determined by the effect of multiple scattering. The values of $a_i$ and $b_i$ were determined as shown in Table \ref{tab:smearing_parameters}.

\subsection{PYTHIA Tuning}

\subsubsection{Monash Tune}
The Monash tune is the standard tuning system used in PYTHIA 8. It utilises the Lund string model. The Lund string model models the colour force between two quarks as a string, with a certain tension. When quarks are separated after a hard scattering, the string is stretched, increasing the energy stored in the string. When the stored energy is large enough, the string breaks, creating a new quark-antiquark pair. This describes the process of hadronisation. This limits the process of hadronisation into baryons to only occur from diquark-antidiquark pair production.

The Monash tune describes the parameters of this Lund string model hadronisation process, defining the flavour ratios, fragmentation functions, and the probability of diquark-antidiquark pair production.

\subsubsection{CR-BLC Mode 2}
The CR-BLC (Colour Reconnection Beyond Leading Colour) model extends the Lund string model. Rather than only allowing strings to form between a quark-antiquark pair, it allows for strings between three quarks as well. This leads to an increased baryon production, increasing the $\Lambda_c^+$ fragmentation fraction. In addition to this, the model can be further modified by only allowing causally connected strings in space-time to reconnect. This is known as CR-BLC Mode 2, and is the standard tuning used when analysing heavy quark fragmentation.

\subsection{Neural Network Architecture}
The neural network was coded using the `tensorflow' package in Python 3.12.12, using the `keras' API.

\subsubsection{Layer Architecture}
The final FFNN architecture was determined through a combination of research and trial and error. The final configuration settled on is outlined in Figure \ref{fig:nn_architecture}. The input layer is comprised of the ten summary variables outlined in Section \ref{sec:summary_variables}. A decreasing layer-width architecture $(128 \rightarrow 64 \rightarrow 32)$ is used for the hidden layers. This ensures that the model is less prone to overfitting, and forces the model to learn the most important high-level features of the data. After each hidden layer, batch normalisation and dropout layers are added. Batch normalisation helps mitigate the problem of internal covariate shift, by normalising the data within each batch. This keeps the inputs in a stable range, increasing training stability and speed. Dropout layers randomly set a subset of the neurons to zero during training, improving generalisation by forcing the model to learn more robust features without relying on specific node connections. Finally, the output layer is comprised of three neurons, corresponding to the three classes: $\Lambda_c^+$, $c$, and light.

For the hidden layers, the `ReLU' activation function was used to avoid the problem of vanishing gradients and to increase the speed of computation. For the output layer, the `softmax' activation was used to convert the raw output of the neurons into a probability distribution over the three classes. The `Adam' optimiser was used due to its adaptive learning rates for each parameter, and its strong ability to converge despite noise in the data.

\subsubsection{Loss Function}
The model was trained using the `categorical cross-entropy' (CCE) loss function, which is a standard loss function for multi-class classification problems. For one sample $i$ with $C$ classes, the loss is defined as:

\begin{equation}
    L_i = -\sum^{C}_{c=1} p_{i,c} \log(\hat{p}_{i,c}),
\end{equation}

where $p_{i,c}$ and $\hat{p}_{i,c}$ are the true and predicted probabilities for sample $i$ and class $c$, respectively. The total loss is the average loss over all samples in the batch. CCE is used as it heavily penalises confident wrong predictions, which is desirable for this classification task.

\subsection{Training}
The model was trained on 10 million events, split between 5 million charm events (including $\Lambda_c^+$ and other charmed hadrons) and 5 million background events (light and bottom quark events). This 50/50 split was decided on due to the low cross-section of charm events compared to background events (around 1:400), which would lead to the NN being biased towards predicting background events. This is taken into consideration when evaluating the results. As the charm events are automatically weighted by cross-section in PYTHIA, this does not need to be done for these events.

The data was split into training and validation sets, with 80\% of the data used for training and 20\% of the data used for validation. For evaluation, a separate test set of 10 million events split in the same way as the training data was used. This is to ensure the model was evaluated on unseen, statistically independent data.

\section{Statistical Analysis}

\subsection{Maximum Likelihood Fit}
To statistically verify the results of the machine learning model, a maximum likelihood fit was performed. The signal strength, $\mu$, is defined as:

\begin{equation}
    \mu = \frac{N_{\Lambda_c^+}}{N_{\Lambda_c^+}^{\text{PYTHIA}}},
\end{equation}

where $N_{\Lambda_c^+}$ is the number of $\Lambda_c^+$ events in the ``observed'' data, and $N_{\Lambda_c^+}^{\text{PYTHIA}}$ is the number of $\Lambda_c^+$ events in the PYTHIA simulation. For data that perfectly follows universality, a value of $\mu = 1$ would be expected, whereas if the fragmentation fraction of $\Lambda_c^+$ is significantly higher than that of other charm hadrons, a value of $\mu > 1$ would be expected.

The maximum likelihood fit finds the most probable value of signal strength that the observed data would give. As each bin in the histogram follows a Poisson distribution. The probability mass function of the Poisson distribution is defined as:

\begin{equation}
    P(n; \lambda) = \frac{\lambda^n e^{-\lambda}}{n!},
\end{equation}

where $\lambda$ is the expected number of events, and $n$ is the observed number of events. The total likelihood is therefore calculated as the product of the PMFs for each bin:

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}(\mu) = \prod_{i=1}^{N} \frac{\lambda_i^{n_i} e^{-\lambda_i}}{n_i!},
\end{equation}

where $\lambda_i$ is the expected number of events in bin $i$, and $n_i$ is the observed number of events in bin $i$. The maximum likelihood estimate of $\mu$ is the value of $\mu$ that maximises the likelihood function. To find this, the negative logarithm of the likelihood function is minimised:

\begin{equation}
    - \ln \mathcal{L}(\mu) = -\sum^{N}_{i=1} \left( n_i \ln \lambda_i - \lambda_i - \ln n_i! \right).
\end{equation}

This is done by iterating over a range of $\mu$ values and calculating the negative log likelihood for each one, and then finding the minimum along that curve. This gives the most likely value of $\mu$ for that dataset. To find the $1\sigma$ errors, the values of $\mu$ corresponding to an increase of 0.5 in the negative log likelihood from the minimum are found.

\subsubsection{Pull Distributions}
To ensure that the maximum likelihood fit is statistically reliable, pull distributions were generated. These show the difference between the fitted signal strength and the true signal strength divided by the uncertainty for many likelihood fits, in this case 10000. If the likelihood is statistically reliable, the pull distribution should be a Gaussian distribution, with a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of 1, via the central limit theorem. If this is not the case, then the maximum likelihood fit will either consistently underestimate or overestimate the signal strength, making the results unreliable.

To show that the model would be able to find a signal strength corresponding to an increase in $\Lambda_c^+$ fragmentation fraction similar to that observed at ALICE, the model was evaluated on two different PYTHIA tunings, one with the Monash tuning simulating fragmentation functions similar to those found at $e^+e^-$ colliders, and one with the CR-BLC Mode 2 tuning simulating fragmentation functions similar to those found by ALICE.
